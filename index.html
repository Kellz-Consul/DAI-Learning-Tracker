Core Concepts
HOW LLMS WORK
Large Language Models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on internet-scale text to predict the next token — a chunk of a word. They don't store facts in a database; they compress patterns from training data into billions of numerical parameters, then use those patterns to reason and generate. When you send Claude a prompt, it processes every token in sequence and generates a response one token at a time. The "context window" is the model's working memory — everything it can see at once: your instructions, conversation history, and any documents you've shared. Claude's context window is up to 200,000 tokens (roughly 150,000 words — an entire novel). Larger context = more nuance, history, and depth Claude can hold simultaneously. Key consulting insight: LLMs don't "look things up" — they reason from patterns. This means they can be confidently wrong (hallucination). Always ground high-stakes outputs in verified data, and treat Claude as a brilliant reasoning partner, not an infallible oracle.
WHY CLAUDE IS DIFFERENTIATED
Claude is built by Anthropic, an AI safety company founded by former OpenAI researchers. Its core architecture includes Constitutional AI — a training method that teaches Claude to be helpful, harmless, and honest by having it critique and revise its own outputs against a set of principles. In practice this means Claude is less likely to produce harmful content, more likely to flag uncertainty, and better at nuanced, balanced analysis. Key differentiators for consulting work: (1) 200K token context window — analyse entire contracts, strategy decks, or datasets in one pass. (2) Strong long-form reasoning — ideal for strategy documents, complex analysis, and structured recommendations. (3) Enterprise safety by default — no training on your inputs, GDPR-aligned, SOC 2 certified. (4) Multimodal — Claude can analyse images, charts, and screenshots alongside text. For Accenture Song work, Claude excels at: synthesising research, drafting client-ready documents, designing agent workflows, and stress-testing strategies. Build a habit of using it daily — the productivity advantage compounds over time.
PROMPT ENGINEERING BASICS
Prompt engineering is the practice of designing inputs to get consistently high-quality outputs from an LLM. The RCTFE framework covers the five elements of a strong prompt: Role ("You are a senior Accenture Song consultant specialising in AI for marketing"), Context (background the model needs — client industry, constraints, audience), Task (what to produce — be specific: "Write a 3-slide narrative" not "write something about AI"), Format (how to structure the output — table, bullet list, executive summary, slide titles), Examples (show one or two ideal outputs to anchor the quality standard). The difference between a weak and strong prompt is almost always specificity and context. Treat prompting like briefing a brilliant but new team member — they need to know who they are, what you need, who it's for, and what good looks like. Build a personal prompt library: save your best prompts for recurring tasks (discovery prep, business case drafting, proposal writing, workshop design) and reuse and iterate them. Over 20 weeks, this library becomes a significant productivity asset that compounds in value.

Core Concepts
CDP vs CRM vs DMP vs DATA WAREHOUSE
These four terms are consistently confused by clients — your ability to explain them clearly in 60 seconds is a genuine consulting differentiator. CRM (Salesforce Sales Cloud, HubSpot): tracks company-side interactions — deals, contacts, account history, pipeline. Structured around what your team does, not what the customer does. CDP (Salesforce Data Cloud, Adobe Real-Time CDP): unifies ALL customer interactions from every touchpoint (website, app, in-store, email, call centre) into a single real-time profile — the AI data layer designed to feed personalisation engines and AI agents. DMP (Lotame, Oracle BlueKai): manages anonymous audience segments for programmatic advertising — cookie-based, third-party data dependent, largely obsolete post-cookie deprecation. Data Warehouse (Snowflake, BigQuery, Redshift): stores large volumes of historical data for analytics and reporting — batch processing, not real-time. The golden rule: CRM is about your relationships, CDP is about your customers' behaviour, DMP is about anonymous audiences, the Data Warehouse is your analytical source of truth. Most enterprise clients have all four and need advisory on what to integrate, what to consolidate, and what to retire — this analysis is often the first deliverable on a MarTech advisory engagement.
HOW AI USES CUSTOMER DATA
AI-powered personalisation and agentic systems need four data ingredients to work. (1) Unified identity — one persistent ID per customer that links their behaviour across devices, channels, and sessions. Without this, AI sees multiple partial people instead of one complete customer. (2) Behavioural signals — what they browse, click, search, add to cart, abandon, purchase, and return. These recency, frequency, and value signals are the fuel for propensity models. (3) Attributes — who they are: demographics, tier, segment, preference history, predicted CLV. (4) Consent — explicit, lawful permission to use data for personalisation and AI decision-making. GDPR and the EU AI Act require this to be specific and withdrawable. The data readiness diagnostic is one of the most valuable things a Song consultant can run in discovery — most clients dramatically overestimate the quality and coverage of their customer data. "Garbage in, garbage out" is the most common reason AI personalisation projects underdeliver — surfacing this honestly before an engagement starts is far better than discovering it mid-implementation.
SALESFORCE DATA CLOUD
Salesforce Data Cloud (rebranded from Customer Data Platform in 2023, also referred to as "Data 360" in late 2025 — same product, evolving branding) is Salesforce's real-time customer data platform. It ingests data from any source — Salesforce CRM, website, mobile app, third-party partners, data warehouses — and creates a unified customer profile that updates in real time. This profile feeds three downstream systems: Einstein AI (predictions — propensity scores, churn risk, next best action recommendations), Agentforce (the agentic layer that takes autonomous actions based on the profile), and Flow (automation workflows). The key enterprise advantage is native integration with the full Salesforce ecosystem — Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Commerce Cloud — meaning data doesn't need to leave the platform. For Song engagements, Data Cloud is typically the foundational data layer recommended for Salesforce-stack clients. Advisory caveat: Data Cloud is powerful but requires significant data engineering investment to unify messy enterprise data — always scope the data migration work carefully and honestly before promising AI outcomes to the client.
ADOBE REAL-TIME CDP
Adobe's Real-Time CDP (part of Adobe Experience Platform — AEP) uses a structured data model called XDM (Experience Data Model) — a standardised schema ensuring data from different sources (web, mobile, CRM, POS) maps to the same structure, making it immediately usable by AI. Identity namespaces link different identifiers (email, cookie ID, loyalty number, CRM ID) into a single unified profile. Sensei AI — Adobe's ML layer — runs continuously on this data to generate predictions: propensity to purchase, churn risk, lifetime value bands, next best experience. These predictions feed Adobe Journey Optimizer (real-time personalised journeys and AI-generated content) and Adobe Target (AI-driven A/B and multivariate testing). Key differentiator over Salesforce: Adobe's IP-indemnified creative AI (Firefly) means the content supply chain — from data to insight to content to activation — can be entirely within one vendor. Best fit: clients already invested in the Adobe stack, B2C brands with complex multi-brand environments, clients prioritising content and creative AI alongside data. Advisory point: ask clients which of their current Adobe AI features they're actually using — most are paying for capabilities they haven't activated.

Core Concepts
PERSONALISATION MATURITY MODEL
The personalisation maturity model is one of the most useful diagnostic frameworks in a Song consultant's toolkit — it rapidly establishes where a client is today and creates a clear roadmap. Level 1 — Rule-based: static rules manually configured by marketers. "If customer is aged 25–35 and in London, show offer X." Fast to implement, zero data science required, but low relevance and high maintenance overhead. Most clients believe they've moved beyond this but haven't. Level 2 — Segment-based: customers clustered into behavioural or demographic segments served segment-specific content. Better than Level 1 but still blunt — a segment of 50,000 people gets the same experience. Level 3 — ML-driven: individual-level predictions drive each customer's experience. Propensity models, collaborative filtering, and real-time signals personalise content, offers, and timing per individual. Requires a CDP, clean data, and ML capability. Level 4 — Real-time agentic: AI agents monitor live customer signals and act autonomously — adjusting price, triggering outreach, personalising in real time without human instruction. This is where Agentforce and agentic commerce operate. Your diagnostic: ask the client for two or three examples of personalisation in action. Within ten minutes you can place them on this model. The gap between where they are and Level 3 is typically your engagement.
NEXT-BEST-ACTION (NBA)
Next-Best-Action is the decision engine that determines, in real time, what communication, offer, or experience to deliver to each individual customer across every channel simultaneously. It's the architecture behind Agentforce, Pega Infinity, Adobe Target, and Salesforce Marketing Cloud Personalization. Four components work in sequence: (1) Eligibility — hard rules that determine if the customer qualifies for this action at all. "Only customers who haven't purchased in 30 days." These are non-negotiable guardrails. (2) Propensity — an ML model scores the likelihood that this customer will respond positively. A churn-risk model, a cross-sell model, a reactivation model — the higher the score, the more relevant the action. (3) Business rules — operational constraints layered on top of propensity: budget caps (no more than A$5 per customer per week in discounts), channel fatigue rules (no more than 3 contacts in 7 days), regulatory constraints. (4) Arbitration — when multiple actions qualify, which wins? Typically the action with the highest expected commercial value (propensity × value of action). NBA is not a single tool you install — it's an architecture you design. Many clients have all the components but haven't connected them into a coherent system. Designing that integration is a high-value Song advisory engagement.
PERSONALISATION ROI BENCHMARKS
McKinsey benchmark: top personalisation performers see 10–15% revenue uplift on average, up to 40% for best-in-class. Key metrics to promise in a business case: conversion rate lift (typically +5–15%), average order value increase (+8–12%), content production cost reduction (50–70% with GenAI), NPS improvement (+5–10 points from relevance). Always present a range: conservative / base / optimistic. Never anchor on the top number alone.

AI CONTENT SUPPLY CHAIN
The content supply chain — from creative brief to live asset — is one of the most expensive and time-consuming operations in marketing. Traditional pipeline: brief (Day 1) → agency kickoff (Day 3) → first draft (Day 10) → internal review → revisions → client approval → final production → live (Day 30). Total: 4–6 weeks, A$20k–A$100k+ per campaign for major brands. AI-powered pipeline: brief (Day 1) → AI generates 10 copy variants + 20 image concepts within hours → human creative director selects and refines the best 2–3 → brand compliance check → live (Day 3). Total: 1–3 days, 60–70% cost reduction. The human role fundamentally shifts: from creator to curator, director, and quality controller. This doesn't eliminate creative jobs — it eliminates repetitive execution work and frees creatives for strategy, craft, and judgment. Song advisory: clients adopting AI content supply chains need three things beyond the technology: (1) updated brand guidelines written for AI (what tone, style, constraints should the AI follow?), (2) a governance process for AI content review, and (3) a change management plan for the creative and marketing team. The technology is the easy part — change management is where most programmes stall.
N8N & WORKFLOW AUTOMATION
n8n is an open-source workflow automation tool (like Zapier but more powerful and self-hostable). Structure: Trigger (new CRM lead, form submission, schedule, webhook) → Nodes (Claude API call, data transform, conditional logic) → Output (send email, update Salesforce, post to Slack, write to Google Sheet). Key advantage: you can call any API including Claude, making it an agentic automation platform. Free to self-host, or use n8n.cloud on a free trial. Recommended for: marketing automation, lead routing, content workflows, reporting pipelines.
POST-COOKIE MEASUREMENT
Third-party cookies — the mechanism powering digital measurement and targeting for 25 years — are largely deprecated. Safari and Firefox blocked them years ago; Chrome's deprecation process is ongoing. This has broken traditional attribution models that relied on following users across the web. Replacement approaches: (1) GA4 with Consent Mode — Google's first-party measurement platform uses behavioural modelling and ML to fill gaps where consent wasn't given. Server-side tagging improves accuracy further. This is now the measurement baseline — every client needs it. (2) Marketing Mix Modelling (MMM) — statistical regression correlating media spend (TV, paid social, search, display) with revenue outcomes over time. Shows true contribution of each channel including those hard to track digitally. Meta's Robyn and Google's Meridian are open-source MMM tools. MMM is experiencing a major enterprise renaissance. (3) Incrementality testing — controlled experiments using holdout groups to measure the true causal lift from a specific campaign or channel. "Would these customers have purchased anyway?" The gold standard for channel effectiveness. (4) Data Clean Rooms — privacy-safe environments where brands and publishers match first-party data without sharing raw records. AWS Clean Rooms, Google Ads Data Hub, Snowflake Data Clean Rooms. Song advisory: GA4 + server-side tagging is the immediate priority for every client. MMM is the medium-term investment for any client spending A$5m+ on media. Incrementality testing should be built into every major campaign from Day 1.

An AI agent is an LLM equipped with the ability to take actions in the world — not just generate text, but do things. The four components that define an agent: (1) LLM — the reasoning engine. Claude receives a task, reasons about what needs to happen, decides which tool to use and with what inputs, and evaluates whether the result achieved the goal. (2) Tools — functions the agent can call. Examples: web_search (get current information), get_customer_profile (look up a customer record in your CRM via API), send_email (trigger a communication), create_document (write to Google Docs), calculate (run a financial model). You define the tools; Claude decides when and how to use them. (3) Memory — what the agent knows and can access: the system prompt (its role and instructions), conversation history, retrieved context from documents or databases (RAG), and results of previous tool calls. (4) Planning loop — the core mechanic. Claude reasons about what to do → selects a tool and calls it → observes the result → reasons about what to do next → repeats. This cycle is called the ReAct pattern (Reason + Act). A single Claude API call returning a text answer is not an agent. An agent runs this loop autonomously over many steps to complete a complex multi-part task. "AI agent" is widely misused as marketing language — when clients say they want one, always probe: do they mean a chatbot (single turn), an automation workflow (n8n), or a true reasoning-and-acting system? They're very different in complexity, cost, and risk.
TOOL USE IN CLAUDE
You define tools as structured schemas: name, description, parameters. Claude reads the descriptions and decides when to call each tool based on context — you don't hard-code "call this tool at step 3." Example tools: web_search (get current information), get_customer_profile (look up a customer in your CRM), send_email (trigger a communication), create_document (write to Google Docs). Claude is unusually good at knowing when NOT to use a tool (when it already knows the answer). Best practice: write clear tool descriptions — Claude uses them to decide when to call.
HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP DESIGN
Critical principle: AI agents should pause for human approval at consequential decision points. Autonomous = fine for: research, drafting, data retrieval, analysis. Requires human approval: sending a communication to a customer, making a purchase, escalating a complaint, updating a CRM record. Design rule: the higher the stakes and the less reversible the action, the more you need a human gate. As an agent builds a track record of reliability, you can progressively remove gates. Start conservative, earn autonomy.

Core Concepts
MULTI-AGENT ARCHITECTURE
Multi-agent systems address the fundamental limitation of single agents: a single agent trying to do everything becomes incoherent and produces lower quality output than focused specialist agents working in coordination. An orchestrator agent receives the top-level task, breaks it into subtasks, delegates each to a specialist agent with the right tools and instructions, collects results, and synthesises the final output. Three primary orchestration patterns: (1) Sequential — Agent A's output is the input to Agent B, which feeds Agent C. Best for content pipelines (research → write → critique → approve) where each step depends on the previous. Predictable and easy to debug but slower. (2) Parallel — Agents A, B, and C run simultaneously on different subtasks; the orchestrator waits for all and synthesises. Best for multi-channel campaign creation (email agent, social agent, display agent working concurrently) or market research across multiple sectors. Faster but harder to orchestrate. (3) Hierarchical — orchestrator delegates to team leads, who each manage worker agents. Best for large, complex projects with multiple workstreams — mirrors how a consulting engagement team is structured. Key design principles: (1) Each agent should have a single, clear responsibility — the "single responsibility principle" from software engineering applies directly. (2) Pass structured outputs between agents — use JSON or markdown tables, not prose. (3) Build error handling into the orchestrator — if a specialist agent fails, the orchestrator should retry or escalate rather than passing the failure downstream.
PROCESS AUTOMATION OPPORTUNITY MAP
High automation potential (automate now): content drafting, lead qualification, data extraction and cleaning, report generation, campaign trafficking and QA, competitor monitoring. Medium potential (automate with oversight): Tier 1 customer service, personalisation recommendations, A/B test management, social listening. Low potential / keep human: creative direction and strategy, relationship management for key accounts, crisis response, board-level communications, any decision with significant reputational risk. Use this map in client discovery to rapidly identify quick wins.
RESPONSIBLE AI FOR MARKETING
Five principles every Song AI engagement must address: (1) Transparency — inform customers when AI is making decisions or generating content. (2) Fairness — test AI outputs for bias in targeting, pricing, and content. (3) Privacy — only use data that is consented, lawfully obtained, and minimised to what's needed. (4) Accountability — maintain a human who can explain and override any AI decision. (5) Safety — define what the agent can and cannot do; build guardrails into the system prompt and tool definitions. EU AI Act (2024): marketing AI is typically "limited risk" — transparency obligations apply. UK GDPR: AI decisions about individuals require human oversight options.

REGULATORY ESSENTIALS FOR MARKETING AI
The regulatory landscape for AI in marketing moved from theoretical to operational in 2024–2025. Every Song consultant advising on AI must understand the essentials — not at legal depth, but sufficiently to flag issues and know when to escalate to legal counsel. EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, entered into force August 2024, phased enforcement to August 2026): AI systems classified into four risk tiers. Unacceptable risk (banned): real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring systems. High risk (conformity assessment + human oversight required): AI in recruitment, credit scoring, access to essential services, law enforcement. (1) Automated decision-making (Article 22): if AI makes decisions that significantly affect individuals (personalised pricing, targeted advertising that materially affects access), the individual has the right to object and request human review. (2) Data minimisation: only process personal data necessary for the specific, stated purpose. Consent for "improving your experience" does not automatically cover AI personalisation. Client action list: conduct an AI use case inventory, classify by EU AI Act risk tier, implement disclosure notices now, review consent scopes with legal counsel, and document human oversight mechanisms.

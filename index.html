Core Concepts
HOW LLMS WORK
Large Language Models predict the next word (token) based on everything before it — trained on internet-scale text. They don't retrieve facts from a database; they reason and generate. A "context window" is the LLM's working memory — Claude's is up to 200K tokens, roughly 150,000 words. Longer context = more document, history, and instruction Claude can hold at once.
WHY CLAUDE IS DIFFERENTIATED
Claude is built by Anthropic with a focus on safety and reliability. Key advantages for consulting work: (1) Constitutional AI — trained to be helpful, harmless, and honest. (2) 200K context window — analyse entire contracts, reports, or datasets. (3) Strong reasoning and nuanced writing — ideal for strategy documents and client communications. (4) Enterprise-grade: no training on your data by default.
PROMPT ENGINEERING BASICS
The RCTFE framework: Role (who Claude should be — "You are a senior Accenture Song consultant"), Context (background the client would give you), Task (what to produce), Format (how to structure the output — bullet list, table, narrative), Examples (show one or two to set the standard). Core Concepts
CDP vs CRM vs DMP vs DATA WAREHOUSE
CRM (e.g. Salesforce Sales Cloud): tracks relationships, sales pipeline, account history — structured around the company's interactions. CDP (e.g. Salesforce Data Cloud, Adobe Real-Time CDP): unifies ALL customer touchpoints into one real-time profile — the AI data layer. DMP (e.g. Lotame): anonymous audience segments for advertising — cookie-based, largely obsolete post-cookie. Data Warehouse (e.g. Snowflake, BigQuery): historical analytics, batch processing, not real-time. Clients often have all four and need advisory on what to consolidate.
HOW AI USES CUSTOMER DATA
AI personalisation needs four ingredients: (1) Unified identity — one ID per customer across all touchpoints. (2) Behavioural signals — what they browse, click, buy, abandon. (3) Attributes — who they are (demographics, segment, tier). (4) Consent — permission to use it. Without clean, unified data, AI personalisation fails before it starts. "Garbage in, garbage out" is the single most common reason AI projects underdeliver.
SALESFORCE DATA CLOUD
Data Cloud ingests data from any source (CRM, website, mobile, partner data) → creates a unified customer profile in real time → connects to Einstein AI for predictions → feeds Agentforce for autonomous action. Renamed from "Customer Data Platform" in 2023, "Data 360" in late 2025 — same product, new branding. Key differentiator: native integration with the full Salesforce ecosystem.
ADOBE REAL-TIME CDP
AEP architecture: XDM schema (Experience Data Model — standardised way to structure customer data) → Identity namespace (links email, cookie, CRM ID into one profile) → Sensei AI (ML predictions — propensity, churn, LTV) → Journey Optimizer (real-time activation). Best fit: clients already on Adobe stack, B2C, complex multi-brand environments. Key differentiator: IP-indemnified AI (Firefly) and content supply chain integration.

Core Concepts
PERSONALISATION MATURITY MODEL
Level 1 — Rule-based: if segment = "high value" show offer X. Fast to implement, low relevance. Level 2 — Segment-based: cluster customers by behaviour, serve segment content. Better, but still blunt. Level 3 — ML-driven: predict what each individual wants, when. Requires clean data and a CDP. Level 4 — Real-time agentic: AI monitors live signals and acts autonomously per customer. Most clients are at Level 1–2 and believe they're at Level 3. Your job: diagnose honestly, then roadmap to Level 3+.
NEXT-BEST-ACTION (NBA)
NBA is the decision engine behind Agentforce, Pega, and Adobe Target. Four components: (1) Eligibility — is this customer in scope for this action? (2) Propensity — ML model scores likelihood to respond. (3) Business rules — channel caps, budget limits, fatigue rules (no more than 3 contacts per week). (4) Arbitration — when multiple actions qualify, which wins? Typically highest expected value. NBA is not a single tool — it's an architecture. Song advises on all four layers.
PERSONALISATION ROI BENCHMARKS
McKinsey benchmark: top personalisation performers see 10–15% revenue uplift on average, up to 40% for best-in-class. Key metrics to promise in a business case: conversion rate lift (typically +5–15%), average order value increase (+8–12%), content production cost reduction (50–70% with GenAI), NPS improvement (+5–10 points from relevance). Always present a range: conservative / base / optimistic. Never anchor on the top number alone.

Core Concepts
WHAT IS AN AI AGENT
An AI agent is an LLM with the ability to take actions. The four components: (1) LLM — the reasoning engine (Claude). (2) Tools — functions the agent can call: search the web, read a file, call an API, write to a database. (3) Memory — what the agent knows: system prompt, conversation history, retrieved context. (4) Planning loop — the agent reasons about what to do, takes an action, observes the result, reasons again. This cycle (Reason → Act → Observe) is called the ReAct pattern. A single Claude API call is not an agent. An agent runs this loop autonomously over many steps.
TOOL USE IN CLAUDE
You define tools as structured schemas: name, description, parameters. Claude reads the descriptions and decides when to call each tool based on context — you don't hard-code "call this tool at step 3." Example tools: web_search (get current information), get_customer_profile (look up a customer in your CRM), send_email (trigger a communication), create_document (write to Google Docs). Claude is unusually good at knowing when NOT to use a tool (when it already knows the answer). Best practice: write clear tool descriptions — Claude uses them to decide when to call.
HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP DESIGN
Critical principle: AI agents should pause for human approval at consequential decision points. Autonomous = fine for: research, drafting, data retrieval, analysis. Requires human approval: sending a communication to a customer, making a purchase, escalating a complaint, updating a CRM record. Design rule: the higher the stakes and the less reversible the action, the more you need a human gate. As an agent builds a track record of reliability, you can progressively remove gates. Start conservative, earn autonomy.
AGENTIC AI USE CASES IN MARKETING & SALES
Content agent: receive brief → research competitors → draft 3 variants → brand-check → return to human for approval. Campaign agent: ingest brief → define audience → generate creative → recommend channels → monitor and optimise. CX agent: receive customer query → identify intent → retrieve relevant data → resolve or escalate. Sales SDR agent: monitor intent signals → personalise outreach → qualify against criteria → book meeting → hand off to rep. Each use case follows the same ReAct architecture — what changes is the tools and business rules.

Core Concepts
CONTENT AGENT ARCHITECTURE
A production-grade content agent has three specialist agents in sequence: (1) Research Agent — given a brief, it searches for competitor positioning, audience insights, relevant stats, and brand guidelines. Tools: web_search, read_document. (2) Writer Agent — given research output, it generates copy in 2–3 variants (email, social, display). Tool: write_document. (3) Critic Agent — scores each variant against brand voice criteria (0–10) and flags compliance issues. Tool: read_guidelines. Human reviews the scored variants and approves. This pipeline produces better output than a single Claude prompt because each agent is specialised and focused.
CX AGENT DESIGN PRINCIPLES
Five components of a well-designed CX agent: (1) Intent classification — what does the customer actually want? (order status / return / complaint / general query). (2) Slot filling — collect the information needed to resolve it (order number, email, date of purchase). (3) Business logic — call your systems (check order status, apply return policy, retrieve account). (4) Response generation — natural language answer, empathetic tone. (5) Escalation logic — when sentiment is negative, issue is complex, or customer requests human. Test with at least 20 simulated conversations before calling it demo-ready.
AGENT FAILURE MODES
Three common failures and their mitigations: (1) Hallucination — agent states wrong information confidently. Fix: use RAG to ground responses in verified data, add confidence thresholds. (2) Scope creep — agent takes actions outside its intended remit. Fix: clear system prompt with explicit boundaries, restricted tool set. (3) Infinite loops — agent repeats the same action without making progress. Fix: max step limits (e.g. 10 iterations), loop detection logic. Document failure modes from testing — they become your governance artefact and show clients you've thought about risk.

Core Concepts
MULTI-AGENT ARCHITECTURE
An orchestrator agent receives a complex task, breaks it into subtasks, and delegates to specialist agents. Patterns: (1) Sequential — Agent A outputs feeds Agent B inputs feeds Agent C. Good for: content pipelines, research → draft → review. (2) Parallel — Agents A, B, and C all work simultaneously, orchestrator synthesises. Good for: multi-channel campaigns, market research across sectors. (3) Hierarchical — orchestrator → team leads → workers. Good for: complex campaigns with multiple workstreams. Key design rule: keep each specialist agent narrow. A focused agent outperforms a general agent on its specific task.
PROCESS AUTOMATION OPPORTUNITY MAP
High automation potential (automate now): content drafting, lead qualification, data extraction and cleaning, report generation, campaign trafficking and QA, competitor monitoring. Medium potential (automate with oversight): Tier 1 customer service, personalisation recommendations, A/B test management, social listening. Low potential / keep human: creative direction and strategy, relationship management for key accounts, crisis response, board-level communications, any decision with significant reputational risk. Use this map in client discovery to rapidly identify quick wins.
RESPONSIBLE AI FOR MARKETING
Five principles every Song AI engagement must address: (1) Transparency — inform customers when AI is making decisions or generating content. (2) Fairness — test AI outputs for bias in targeting, pricing, and content. (3) Privacy — only use data that is consented, lawfully obtained, and minimised to what's needed. (4) Accountability — maintain a human who can explain and override any AI decision. (5) Safety — define what the agent can and cannot do; build guardrails into the system prompt and tool definitions. EU AI Act (2024): marketing AI is typically "limited risk" — transparency obligations apply. UK GDPR: AI decisions about individuals require human oversight options.

RISK-ADJUSTED ROI
Three risks to quantify in every AI business case: (1) Hallucination risk — if the AI produces wrong outputs, what's the cost? (reputational damage, compliance breach, customer compensation). Mitigation: human review gates, RAG grounding. (2) Adoption risk — if the team doesn't use it, what's wasted? (sunk implementation cost). Mitigation: change management budget, champion network, phased rollout. (3) Regulatory risk — if the regulator fines or requires remediation (GDPR, EU AI Act). Mitigation: governance framework, legal review. Apply a risk discount to the base case: conservative = 50% of base benefits in Year 1, scaling to 80% by Year 3.
REGULATORY ESSENTIALS FOR MARKETING AI
EU AI Act (in force 2024, enforcement phased to 2026): AI systems are classified by risk. Marketing AI (personalisation, content generation, chatbots) is generally "limited risk" — obligation is transparency: tell users when they're interacting with AI. "High risk" (credit scoring, recruitment, biometric ID) requires conformity assessment and human oversight. UK GDPR: automated decision-making that significantly affects individuals (targeting, pricing) requires: a legal basis, the right to object, and human review on request. Practical advice to clients: document your AI use cases, classify them by risk level, and implement disclosure notices now — before regulators come asking.
